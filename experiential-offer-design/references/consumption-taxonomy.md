# Consumption Taxonomy for Experiential Offer Design

Use this reference when the offer type matters. The goal is to avoid treating a concert, a jacket, a journal, a SaaS tool, a supplement, and a coaching service as if they need the same experiential layer.

## Core Terminology

### Experiential purchase

The buyer mainly pays to live an event or series of events.

Examples: trip, concert, dinner, retreat, workshop, live course.

Design implications:
- Focus on anticipation, peak moments, memory, social connection, and story.
- Reduce risk with clarity, scheduling flexibility, expectations, and proof.
- Create artifacts after the experience: recap, photos, certificate, next milestone.

### Material purchase

The buyer mainly pays to own a tangible object.

Examples: clothing, phone, furniture, journal, tool, decoration.

Design implications:
- Focus on ownership story, unboxing, first use, recurring ritual, care, display, identity, and long-term relationship.
- The object can become a passport to content, community, progress, or a repeated ritual.
- Tangibility matters more for constrained buyers because it can signal control and lasting value.

### Hybrid offer

The offer combines object, service, content, community, software, or ritual.

Examples: fitness bike + classes, journal + onboarding emails, skincare product + routine, camera + app + challenge.

Design implications:
- Identify the core object/service, then design the experience stack around it.
- Make each layer earn its place: object, digital onboarding, community, content, progress, support.
- The best hybrid offers make the object feel like the entry point to a larger transformation.

## Goods vs Services

This is an economic delivery-form taxonomy, not the same as experiential vs material purchase.

### Goods

Tangible items transferred to the buyer.

Design implications:
- Use packaging, physical inserts, sensory details, care rituals, setup flows, and owner-only content.
- For goods, the delivery moment is part of the product experience.

### Services

Actions performed for or with the buyer.

Design implications:
- Choreograph the delivery: intake, preparation, kickoff, live interaction, handoff, follow-up.
- Make intangible value tangible through plans, summaries, dashboards, recaps, milestones, or artifacts.
- Trust and proof matter because quality may be hard to evaluate upfront.

### Digital products and software

Intangible but interactive products.

Design implications:
- Use onboarding, progress, personalization, feedback, templates, milestones, micro-interactions, and lifecycle messages.
- Replace physical unboxing with first-run experience.

## Durability and Use Pattern

### Durable goods

Used over a long period.

Examples: furniture, devices, equipment.

Best levers:
- Setup ritual
- Care and maintenance ritual
- Owner identity
- Long-term progress or upgrades
- Living product drops

### Semi-durable goods

Used for a while, often worn or displayed.

Examples: clothing, accessories, home decor, notebooks.

Best levers:
- Identity and self-expression
- Fit, feel, style, display, collection
- First wear / first use ritual
- Care guide and owner story

### Non-durable / consumable goods

Used up quickly or repeatedly.

Examples: food, drinks, cosmetics, supplements, capsules.

Best levers:
- Ritualized choice
- Sensory signature
- Replenishment rhythm
- Habit loop
- Bundles and seasonal drops

### One-off experiences

Consumed once, often scheduled.

Examples: event, dinner, workshop, travel activity.

Best levers:
- Anticipation
- Clear agenda
- Peak moment
- Memory artifact
- Referral/UGC moment

### Recurring services or subscriptions

Repeated delivery over time.

Examples: coaching, SaaS, membership, subscription box.

Best levers:
- First 30 days
- Progress visibility
- Regular milestones
- Community rhythm
- Retention rituals

## Search / Experience / Credence Goods

This taxonomy is about when quality can be evaluated. Do not confuse "experience goods" with "experiential purchases."

### Search goods

Quality can be evaluated before purchase.

Examples: dimensions, specs, visible style, clear features.

Design implications:
- Make comparison easy.
- Use demos, specs, transparent inclusions, photos, and direct feature-to-benefit mapping.

### Experience goods

Quality is mostly known after purchase or use.

Examples: taste, comfort, course quality, hospitality, most services.

Design implications:
- Reduce uncertainty with trials, samples, previews, reviews, demos, guarantees when true, and expectation setting.
- Show the first-use path clearly.

### Credence goods

Quality is hard to evaluate even after purchase.

Examples: legal, health, security, expert consulting, some technical services.

Design implications:
- Lead with trust, credentials, process transparency, diagnostics, evidence, third-party validation, and risk controls.
- Avoid vague transformation claims unless strongly evidenced.

## Practical Classification Questions

Ask these before designing the experiential layer:

1. Is the buyer mainly paying to own something, live something, or access an ongoing system?
2. Is the offer delivered as a good, service, digital product, or hybrid?
3. Is usage one-off, recurring, durable, semi-durable, or consumable?
4. Can the buyer judge quality before purchase, only after use, or not even easily after use?
5. Is the product worn, displayed, stored, consumed, used privately, or used socially?
6. What can become a ritual: choosing, opening, setting up, using, sharing, caring, progressing, replenishing?
7. What proof artifact can make the experience feel tangible?

## Design Selection Cheatsheet

If it is mainly experiential:
- Strengthen anticipation, peak moment, memory, social proof, and post-experience artifact.

If it is mainly material durable:
- Strengthen setup, owner identity, care ritual, upgrades, and living product layer.

If it is worn:
- Strengthen identity, fit confidence, styling ritual, first-wear moment, social visibility, and care.

If it is stored or displayed:
- Strengthen display, collection, environment, object story, and visual distinctiveness.

If it is consumable:
- Strengthen ritualized choice, sensory signature, replenishment rhythm, and habit loop.

If it is a service:
- Strengthen process transparency, milestones, handoffs, tangible artifacts, and follow-up.

If it is digital:
- Strengthen first-run experience, progress, personalization, feedback loops, and lifecycle emails.

If it is an experience good:
- Strengthen preview, trial, expectation setting, reviews, first-use guidance, and risk reversal.

If it is a credence good:
- Strengthen trust architecture, evidence, credentials, diagnostics, process clarity, and ethical claim discipline.
