# Experiential Offer Design Pattern Library

Use this only when the task needs concrete inspiration, product mechanics, or examples. These are pattern references, not proof claims. Do not claim a brand result, metric, or market fact unless the user supplied or verified it.

## 1) Behavior Catalyst

Use when the product should make the user do something more often or more intensely: explore, create, train, relax, connect.

Design moves:
- Shape the form factor around the desired behavior.
- Remove steps that interrupt the moment.
- Make the product easy to use in the real environment where the behavior happens.
- Turn the desired behavior into the identity story: "I am the kind of person who does this."

Digital assets:
- Landing section: "What this helps you do."
- Demo script: show the behavior, not the specs.
- UGC prompt: "Show the moment you used it to ___".

## 2) Product as Stage

Use when the physical product can become a stage for content, coaching, community, or progress.

Design moves:
- Make the product physically presentable in the user's home, workspace, or routine.
- Add a screen, printed guide, QR flow, companion content, or progress surface.
- Turn each session into a guided scene with a clear start and finish.

Digital assets:
- Onboarding email: "Your first session."
- Landing section: "The ritual in 3 steps."
- Visual direction: real environment, not isolated product render.

## 3) Sequenced Ritual

Use when the product has setup, assembly, learning, collecting, or repeated use.

Design moves:
- Break use into chapters: Day 1, Week 1, Level 1, Step 1.
- Add mini-wins early.
- Design one "aha moment" where progress becomes visible.
- Include a simple tracker, card, or booklet so progress feels tangible.

Digital assets:
- Post-purchase sequence: day 0, day 3, day 7, day 30.
- Micro-experience: challenge, checklist, progress quiz.
- UGC prompt: "Show your progress after 7 days."

## 4) Premium Micro-Gesture

Use when the category is commoditized and small sensory details can carry perceived value.

Design moves:
- Design the first 60 seconds of unboxing as a sequence: reveal, touch, pull, peel, open.
- Choose one sensory signature: click, texture, weight, closure, light, sound, scent.
- Make the first-use instruction feel like a ritual, not a manual.

Digital assets:
- Product page: show the unboxing sequence.
- Email day 0: guide the first use.
- Visual direction: close-up gestures and tactile details.

## 5) Ritualized Choice

Use when the product involves choosing a variant, flavor, module, mode, accessory, or setup.

Design moves:
- Turn selection into a named moment.
- Use visual codes for each option.
- Give each option a simple emotional or situational role.
- Add accessories that stage the ritual.

Digital assets:
- Quiz: "Which mode fits your moment?"
- Product page: choice guide.
- Post-purchase card: "Choose today's ___".

## 6) Living Product

Use when the product can evolve after purchase.

Design moves:
- Add content drops, templates, modules, seasonal uses, accessories, or software updates.
- Announce improvements as part of the ownership experience.
- Add one small delight feature that is not purely utilitarian but creates sharing.

Digital assets:
- Monthly owner email.
- "New this month" page section.
- Referral prompt tied to the new drop.

## 7) Passport to Community

Use when the product can unlock belonging, shared practice, or status.

Design moves:
- Make ownership give access to a club, challenge, private content, event, group, or expert session.
- Add a visible but tasteful badge of belonging.
- Create recurring community moments that do not require a store.

Digital assets:
- Community invite email.
- Member-only challenge page.
- UGC prompt: "Share your setup / ritual / result."

## 8) Risk-Control Layer

Use when buyers are budget-constrained or worried about wasting money.

Design moves:
- Make the scope, schedule, and expected outcome clear.
- Add modular entry points.
- Include a tangible artifact that lasts: guide, tracker, toolkit, checklist.
- Offer flexible rescheduling, cancellation, warranty, or guarantee only if true.

Digital assets:
- FAQ and objection handling.
- Transparent pricing section.
- Proof request plan: reviews, pilot outcomes, before/after examples.

## 9) Access Scarcity

Use only when the target is premium/affluent and scarcity is real. For the full framework, see [luxury-status-scarcity.md](luxury-status-scarcity.md).

Design moves:
- Anchor scarcity in a real constraint: seats, dates, expert time, production capacity, access, provenance, or personalization.
- Make the buyer feel they secured access, not just bought a product.
- Preserve aura by avoiding overproduction, constant discounts, or fake urgency.

Digital assets:
- Landing section: "What access includes and why it is limited."
- Checkout microcopy: "Your slot / edition / access is reserved."
- Post-purchase email: private next step, preparation, or owner-only invitation.
