## Core Copywriting Principles for OG Images

Adapted from the copywriting skill. These principles are universal but apply with extra intensity to OG images because of the extreme attention constraints.

### Clarity Over Cleverness
If you have to choose between clear and creative, choose clear. OG images get 1-2 seconds — clever wordplay that requires a second read will fail.

### Benefits Over Features
- Feature: "AI-powered matching algorithm"
- Benefit: "Get your lost items back faster"
The og:image shows the OUTCOME, not the mechanism.

### Specificity Over Vagueness
- Vague: "Better results for your team"
- Specific: "Cut reporting time from 4 hours to 15 minutes"
Numbers, timeframes, and concrete outcomes outperform abstract claims.

### Simple Words, Always
Large-scale experiments confirm: simpler writing (common words, higher readability) correlates with higher click-through. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate."

### One Idea, One Image
Each OG image advances ONE argument. Not three features, not a feature comparison, not a bulleted list. One clear promise.

## Headline Formulas

These work for the primary text overlay on an OG image. Keep to 5-8 words maximum.

### Outcome + Context
Pattern: "[Achieve outcome] [with/for context]"
- "Recover lost items faster"
- "Ship code without breaking things"
- "Create proposals in minutes"

Why it works: Simple, concrete, low cognitive load. Processing fluency maximized.

### Pain → Relief
Pattern: "[Pain point]? [Solution]" or "Turn [bad] into [good]"
- "Lost your keys? Get them back."
- "Turn lost into returned"
- "Stop chasing approvals"

Why it works: Emotionally legible, scanning-friendly. Loss aversion activates engagement.

### Forward Reference (Curiosity)
Pattern: "This is why..." / "The one thing..." / "What [entity] does..."
- "This is why people return things"
- "The one detail finders need"
- "What top teams do differently"

Why it works: Zeigarnik effect — open loop creates tension to resolve.
GUARDRAIL: Page must pay off the curiosity within 5 seconds of landing.

### Numbers
Pattern: "[Number] [things] to [outcome]"
- "3 steps to get it back"
- "7 templates for faster proposals"
- "5 minutes to set up"

Why it works: Numbers increase click-through in large-scale experiments. They signal structure and specificity.
GUARDRAIL: Only use meaningful, honest numbers. Invented precision harms trust.

### Question (Rhetorical)
Pattern: "[Pain-point question]?"
- "Tired of losing things?"
- "Still managing reports in Excel?"
- "What if returns were effortless?"

Why it works: Questions engage readers and make them think about their own situation. Self-referential processing deepens engagement.

### Negative Frame
Pattern: "Stop [bad thing]" / "Don't [miss/lose/waste]"
- "Stop losing revenue to churn"
- "Don't let lost items stay lost"
- "Never rewrite onboarding emails again"

Why it works: Loss aversion — the pain of losing motivates more than the pleasure of gaining.

## Hook Formulas (Adapted from Social Content Skill)

These hooks work well when the OG image accompanies a blog post or content piece.

### Curiosity Hooks
- "I was wrong about [common belief]."
- "The real reason [outcome] happens."
- "[Result] — in [surprisingly short time]."

### Value Hooks
- "How to [outcome] without [pain]"
- "[Number] [things] that [outcome]"
- "Stop [mistake]. Do this instead."

### Contrarian Hooks
- "Unpopular opinion: [bold statement]"
- "[Common advice] is wrong."
- "I stopped [practice] and [result]."

### Story Hooks (for founder/brand content)
- "Last week, [unexpected thing] happened."
- "3 years ago, [past state]. Today, [current state]."

## CTA Patterns for OG Images

OG images don't need traditional CTAs (the click IS the CTA). But label-style micro-CTAs can work.

### When to use a micro-CTA
- Content pieces with a specific deliverable (checklist, guide, template)
- Product pages where the action is clear

### Patterns that work (label-style, not button-style)
- "Get the checklist"
- "See the guide"
- "Try it free"
- "View the map"

### Patterns that trigger banner blindness (AVOID)
- Fake buttons with rounded corners and shadows
- "CLICK HERE" or "LEARN MORE"
- Bright colored CTA blocks
- Multiple CTAs

## Copy by Use Case

### SaaS / Product Page
**Primary:** Outcome promise (what the user gets)
**Secondary:** Product credibility cue (UI screenshot, logo)
- "Build faster without breaking things"
- "Customer support that scales"

### Blog Post / Content
**Primary:** Curiosity or value hook
**Secondary:** Content format label ("Guide", "Case Study", "Tutorial")
- "The surprising reason teams fail at remote work"
- "A step-by-step guide to API security"

### Landing Page
**Primary:** Verbatim match to the landing page headline (message repetition = higher CTR)
**Secondary:** One trust cue
- Mirror the exact promise of the page

### E-commerce / Product
**Primary:** Product image
**Secondary:** One value cue (price, discount, benefit) ONLY if readable when small
- Let the product visual do the work

### Event / Webinar
**Primary:** Event name + key benefit
**Secondary:** Date/time + speaker credibility
- "Design Systems Workshop — March 15"

## Anti-Patterns

### Text anti-patterns
- Full sentences (too long for preview scanning)
- Jargon and technical terms the audience doesn't use
- Multiple competing claims in one image
- Exclamation points (screams "ad")
- ALL CAPS for more than 2-3 words

### Visual-copy mismatch
- Image shows a team meeting, text says "API documentation" → confusing
- Image shows a chart, text says "Simplify your workflow" → disconnected
- Always ensure the visual and the text tell the SAME story

### Message mismatch across metadata
- og:image says "Free template", og:title says "How to build X" → inconsistent
- og:image says "For developers", landing page says "For everyone" → trust broken
- Field experiments confirm: verbatim message repetition across elements > introducing new messages
