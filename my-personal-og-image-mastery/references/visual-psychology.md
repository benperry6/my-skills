## Processing Fluency

**Principle:** People evaluate things more positively when they're easier to process. Ease = positive affect = action.

**OG image application:**
- High figure–ground contrast = instant readability = more clicks
- Symmetry and clean layouts feel "right" subconsciously
- Familiar patterns (recognizable brand elements) process faster
- Simple language in overlays reduces cognitive load
- Repetition of core message across og:title + og:image increases fluency

**Design rules derived:**
- One focal point, one message, one brand cue — nothing more
- Use common words, not jargon
- Match og:image text to og:title keywords (verbatim repetition outperforms paraphrasing in field experiments)

## Banner Blindness

**Principle:** Users automatically ignore elements that look like advertisements. This is learned avoidance, not conscious decision.

**OG image triggers to AVOID:**
- Fake CTA buttons (rounded rectangles with "Click Here")
- Multiple badges, stickers, or promotional overlays
- Stock photography (especially staged smiling faces)
- Bright, cluttered designs with multiple competing elements
- Heavy use of red/yellow "sale" aesthetics

**What works instead:**
- Editorial/content-like design (looks like information, not an ad)
- Product screenshots or authentic photos
- Clean typography on minimal backgrounds
- Designs that feel like content previews, not banner ads

## Mere Exposure Effect

**Principle:** People prefer things they've seen before. Familiarity breeds liking and trust.

**OG image application:**
- Use consistent brand visual system across all OG images (same fonts, colors, layout grid)
- Build a template system so every share reinforces brand recognition
- Consistency across pages matters more than individual creativity
- Over time, people will click YOUR previews because they recognize and trust the pattern

**Template system elements to keep consistent:**
- Background style (same gradient or brand colors)
- Font family and weight
- Layout positioning
- Logo/branding placement (same corner)
- Category badge style

## Loss Aversion & Framing

**Principle:** Losses feel ~2x as painful as equivalent gains feel good. How something is presented (frame) changes how it's perceived.

**OG image application:**
- "Don't miss out" framing outperforms "You could gain"
- "Stop losing X" > "Start gaining X" for urgency
- Frame in terms of what they'll lose by NOT clicking
- "90% success rate" feels different than "10% failure rate" — always frame positively for capabilities, negatively for what happens without your product

**Headline patterns:**
- "Stop [losing/wasting/missing] [thing]"
- "Don't let [bad outcome] happen"
- "[X people] already [got the benefit] — are you next?"

## Contrast Effect

**Principle:** Things seem different depending on what they're compared to.

**OG image application:**
- Before/after imagery is extremely powerful
- Show the "pain state" vs "solution state" visually
- Dark OG images pop against light-background feeds (Facebook, LinkedIn)
- Your OG image is always seen IN CONTEXT of surrounding feed content — design for contrast with the feed, not in isolation

**Design implication:** When designing, always preview your OG image inside a mock feed (light bg + dark bg) to see if it stands out.

## Social Proof

**Principle:** People follow what others are doing. Popularity signals quality and safety.

**OG image application (use sparingly to avoid clutter):**
- Customer count ("Trusted by 10,000+ teams") — only if true and impressive
- Notable logos — only if recognizable to your audience
- Star ratings — compact and scannable
- "Featured in" badges — adds authority

**Rules:**
- Maximum ONE social proof element per OG image
- It should reinforce, not compete with, the primary message
- Never fabricate numbers or testimonials

## Scarcity & Urgency

**Principle:** Limited availability increases perceived value. Scarcity signals desirability.

**OG image application:**
- Time-limited offers: add a deadline or countdown cue
- Exclusive access: "Early access" or "Limited spots"
- Only use when GENUINE — fake scarcity destroys trust

**When appropriate:**
- Product launches with actual limited availability
- Events with capacity constraints
- Seasonal promotions

**When to avoid:**
- Evergreen content (blog posts, feature pages)
- Always-available products
- SaaS with unlimited seats

## Zeigarnik Effect (Curiosity)

**Principle:** Unfinished tasks and open loops occupy the mind. People want to resolve uncertainty.

**OG image application:**
- Forward reference ("This is why...", "The one thing...", "What happens when...")
- Partial reveals that promise resolution behind the click
- Questions that the reader wants answered

**Critical guardrail:** The landing page MUST pay off the curiosity quickly. Curiosity without payoff = high bounce rate + broken trust.

**Headline patterns:**
- "We tested [X]. Here's what happened."
- "The [surprising/counterintuitive] reason [outcome]"
- "What [impressive entity] does differently"

## Hick's Law

**Principle:** Decision time increases with the number and complexity of choices. More options = slower decisions = abandonment.

**OG image application:**
- ONE message per image (not three competing claims)
- ONE visual focal point (not a collage)
- ONE action implied (click to learn more)
- Remove every element that doesn't directly serve comprehension

**The test:** If someone sees your OG image for 1.5 seconds, can they understand the single promise? If not, simplify.

## Anchoring

**Principle:** The first number people see heavily influences subsequent judgments.

**OG image application (for pricing/product pages):**
- Show the higher price first if comparing
- "Was $X, now $Y" anchors the perceived value
- Large numbers of users/customers anchor credibility

**Caution:** Anchoring works in feeds where numbers are visible, but remember Apple/Google say avoid text-heavy images. Use anchoring primarily for social-first platforms (LinkedIn, Facebook, Twitter/X).

---

## Peak-End Rule

**Principle:** People judge experiences by the peak moment and the ending, not the average.

**OG image application:**
- The og:image IS often the "peak moment" of the sharing experience
- A great OG image creates a memorable peak that influences whether the person shares again
- Design for "share-worthiness" — would someone be proud to have this appear in their feed?

## Quick Decision Matrix

| Situation | Primary Psychology | Design Implication |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Low brand awareness | Mere Exposure | Consistent template system |
| Competitive feed | Contrast Effect | Dark bg, high contrast |
| Content marketing | Zeigarnik/Curiosity | Forward reference headline |
| Product page | Processing Fluency | UI screenshot + simple promise |
| Promotional offer | Loss Aversion + Scarcity | "Don't miss" + genuine deadline |
| Trust-building | Social Proof | One credibility signal |
| Complex product | Hick's Law | Simplify to one benefit |
